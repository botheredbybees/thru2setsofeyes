### 2.5 Exhibition Guide

---

#### Purpose

The Exhibition Guide covers everything a facilitator needs to set up, maintain, and close a *Through Two Sets of Eyes* public exhibition across eight weeks. It assumes no prior experience with public art installation.

The exhibition is not a nice addition to the project - it is the project. The weekly act of placing paired images in a public space, where the community can watch a dialogue forming without yet knowing who is speaking, is the mechanism through which the project produces its community-level effects. A project that skips or significantly compromises the exhibition is a different project.

---

#### Section 1: Choosing Your Exhibition Venue

The right venue is the single most important logistical decision in the project. Get this wrong and the exhibition reaches nobody. Get it right and the community becomes an audience that grows week by week.

**What you are looking for**

A good exhibition venue has five characteristics:

*Foot traffic.* Both cohorts' community should pass through it regularly - ideally daily. A space that only older adults visit, or that young people actively avoid, defeats the purpose. The ideal venue is genuinely shared community territory.

*Visibility.* Images should be visible without the viewer having to make a deliberate decision to look. A window facing a footpath or carpark, a noticeboard beside a checkout, a wall beside a café counter - anywhere that catches a passing eye rather than requiring a deliberate detour.

*Weather protection.* Prints need to survive eight weeks without curling, fading, or being damaged by rain. An indoor wall, a covered outdoor space, or a window display are all suitable. An open outdoor noticeboard in a wet climate is not.

*Accessibility.* The exhibition should be reachable by people with mobility aids and visible from a standing or seated position. If the venue has a step at the entrance, note this in your site registration and consider whether a ramp or alternative entrance is available.

*Permission.* You need written confirmation from the venue owner or manager before Week 1. A one-page venue agreement template is in Appendix G of the Facilitator Guide. Most venues will say yes - the exhibition costs them nothing and brings people through the door.

**Finding your venue**

Start with the most obvious shared community space in your area - a supermarket, a library foyer, a community hall entrance, a post office, a hardware store. These are spaces where people of all ages and backgrounds pass through without it being a deliberate cultural choice.

If your first choice says no, ask who else they would suggest. Community business owners usually know each other and will often make an introduction.

**The Cygnet model as a worked example**

The pilot project in Cygnet, Tasmania used the window of a locally owned supermarket - Farah's IGA - as its exhibition space. The window faces the main street, is passed by virtually every resident doing weekly shopping, and is protected from weather. The owner provided the space at no cost in exchange for acknowledgement in the exhibition signage. This is the model - a genuinely communal space with natural foot traffic from both cohorts, offered by a local business or organisation with a stake in community wellbeing.

Your equivalent might be a different kind of business, a library, a community centre, a school foyer, a church hall, or a community garden shed. The principle is the same: find the space where your whole community already goes.

**Contingency venue**

Before Week 1 you must have a contingency venue confirmed - a second space that could host the exhibition if the primary venue becomes unavailable. It does not need to be as good as the primary venue. It just needs to exist and be confirmed in writing. If you reach Week 3 without a contingency venue, stop and find one before running Week 4.

#### Section 2: Print Specifications

**Image size**

A4 (210 x 297mm) is the recommended print size. Large enough to be seen clearly from a metre away, small enough to print affordably on a home or library printer, and a standard size that requires no custom framing or mounting.

If your venue has a large window or wall and you have access to a printer that handles A3 (297 x 420mm), A3 prints make a stronger visual impact. Do not mix sizes within a site - choose one size and use it consistently across all eight weeks.

**Paper**

Matte photo paper, 180-200gsm, is the recommended stock. It prints well on both inkjet and laser printers, does not glare under artificial light, and survives handling without scuffing as badly as gloss stock.

Plain office paper (80gsm) is acceptable if budget or printer access is a constraint. Prints on office paper should be mounted on card before display to prevent curling.

Do not laminate prints - lamination makes images difficult to remove cleanly from the wall and adds unnecessary cost.

**Print sources**

In order of preference:

1. A local print shop or pharmacy photo printer - supports local business, good quality, usually available same-day
2. A library printer - low cost, accessible, adequate quality for A4
3. A home inkjet printer with photo paper - good quality if ink is fresh
4. An online print service with next-day delivery - good quality but requires planning ahead

Budget approximately $1-2 per print for a local print shop, less for library or home printing. Each week produces one print per participant across both cohorts - for a full site of sixteen participants, that is sixteen prints per week, 128 prints across the project. At $2 per print the total print budget is approximately $256 - this is the largest single project cost and should be noted in any funding application.

**Image preparation**

Before sending to print, images should be:

- Cropped to the correct aspect ratio for the chosen print size - 3:4 portrait for A4, or left uncropped if the participant prefers
- Checked for any incidentally identifiable people who appear as the primary subject - crop or adjust if necessary per the public photography ethics agreement
- Not colour-corrected, filtered, or otherwise altered - the image the participant submitted is the image that goes on the wall

The project coordinator performs this check during the weekly submission review. Facilitators do not need to prepare images for print - the coordinator sends print-ready files.

*Design note for V1.0:* In V1.0 the project coordinator prepares and distributes print-ready files to facilitators by email each week. Facilitators take the files to their local print source. This is a manual process acknowledged as a maintenance burden and will be partially automated in V2.0.

#### Section 3: Installation

**Layout principle**

Each week's installation pairs one image from the youth cohort with one image from the older adult cohort, side by side, in response to the same prompt. The pairing is the core visual statement of the project - two perspectives on the same question, placed in conversation without explanation.

Pairing logic:
- One youth image beside one older adult image, per participant pair
- Pairs are arranged by week, accumulating left to right or top to bottom as space allows
- Within a week, the order of pairs is at the facilitator's discretion - there is no correct arrangement
- The same participant's images should appear in the same position each week if possible, so regular viewers can track individual voices across the project

**What goes on the wall each week**

For each image:
- The print itself
- A small label below the image showing: the week number, the prompt text, and the caption the participant submitted
- No name, cohort identification, or any other attribution until Week 8

The label should be printed on plain paper and cut to size - approximately 100 x 50mm. A label template is in Appendix H of the Facilitator Guide.

**Signage**

The exhibition needs three pieces of permanent signage installed in Week 1 and left in place for the duration:

*Title panel:* Project name, a one-paragraph plain-language description of what the exhibition is and how it works, and a note that authorship will be revealed at the Week 8 closing event. Template in Appendix I.

*QR code panel:* A printed QR code linking to the project gallery on the WordPress site, where visitors can see all submitted images from all sites. Label: "See more images from this project and others around the world." A4 size, positioned beside the title panel.

*Venue acknowledgement:* A small panel thanking the venue by name. Template in Appendix J.

**Fixing methods**

Use only removable fixing methods - blue tack, removable adhesive strips, or clip frames. Do not use permanent adhesive, drawing pins, or staples on venue surfaces without explicit written permission from the venue owner.

Clip frames (A4 or A3 clip frames from a discount variety store, approximately $3-5 each) are the recommended display method for venues where the prints need to look polished. They are reusable, require no adhesive, and allow prints to be swapped each week without damaging the frame or the wall.

For window displays, static cling film or suction cup hangers are appropriate alternatives.

**Installation timing**

Prints should be installed within 48 hours of the session at which images were selected. The project rhythm is:

- Session day: participants select images, submit via Google Form
- Day after session: project coordinator reviews submissions, sends print-ready files to facilitator
- Within 48 hours of session: facilitator prints and installs

In practice for a weekly session this means installation happens within two to three days of the session, leaving four to five days before the next session for the community to view the new additions.

#### Section 4: The Week 8 Closing Event

The Week 8 closing event is the pivot point of the entire project. It should be simple, low-key, and participant-led. It is not a performance or a formal presentation.

**What needs to happen**

Before the event:
- Attribution labels are added to every image in the exhibition - each label updated to show the participant's first name and cohort. A label template is in Appendix K.
- Post-project SWEMWBS forms are distributed and collected - this can happen at the event or at the final separate cohort sessions immediately before it, whichever is logistically easier
- Reflection cards are distributed and collected at the event

At the event:
- Both cohorts arrive at the exhibition space together for the first time
- No structured program is required - the exhibition does the work
- The facilitator's role is to welcome people, make introductions if participants want them, and hold the space
- Light refreshments are strongly recommended - they give people something to do with their hands and extend the time people stay

Duration: allow 60-90 minutes. People will leave when they are ready.

**What not to do**

- Do not organise speeches, presentations, or formal proceedings
- Do not pressure participants to interact with the other cohort - let it happen naturally
- Do not remove any images before the event to tidy the exhibition - the accumulation of all eight weeks is the point
- Do not invite media without explicit consent from all participants, obtained in writing before the event

**After the event**

The exhibition may remain in place at the venue's discretion after Week 8. If it remains, add a small panel noting that the project has concluded and directing viewers to the WordPress site for more information.

All printed images belong to the participants who made them. At the close of the project, facilitators should offer prints to participants. Unclaimed prints may be retained by the venue, donated to a local archive, or respectfully disposed of - not uploaded or redistributed without participant consent.

#### Section 5: If Something Goes Wrong

**The venue becomes unavailable mid-project**

Activate your contingency venue immediately. Move all installed images to the new venue as soon as access is available. Notify participants at the next session. Note the venue change in your session notes and update your site registration on the WordPress site.

If no contingency venue is available, pause the physical exhibition while you find one - do not skip weeks permanently. A one-week gap in the physical exhibition is recoverable. Abandoning the physical exhibition is not.

**Images are damaged or go missing**

Reprint from the original file. The project coordinator retains all submitted image files. Contact them with the site code, week number, and participant code of the missing image.

**A participant objects to another participant's image**

Take the objection seriously and privately. If the objecting participant can articulate a specific safeguarding or consent concern, remove the image immediately and consult the project coordinator. If the objection is a matter of personal taste or disagreement - one participant dislikes another's image or caption - acknowledge the feeling and explain that editorial control belongs to the image's creator. Do not remove images on aesthetic grounds.

**The exhibition attracts negative community attention**

Some images - particularly Week 7 advocacy images - may generate community disagreement. This is not a failure. It is the project working. Your role is not to defend individual images but to explain the project's purpose: these are community members showing you what they see. Direct any sustained negative attention to the project coordinator.

That's Section 2.5. A few things worth flagging:

The print preparation and distribution step in V1.0 puts a real weekly workload on you as project coordinator - reviewing every submission and emailing print-ready files to facilitators. Worth thinking about whether that's sustainable if more than two or three sites run simultaneously. It's the right design for V1.0 but the automation priority for V2.0 is clear.

The Week 8 closing event guidance deliberately says very little about what actually happens - because the less structured it is the better it tends to work. Worth a note in the facilitator guide that silence and slow looking are fine outcomes.
