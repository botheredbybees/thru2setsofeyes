### 2.7 Data Submission Guide

---

#### Purpose

This guide explains how facilitators submit project data to the central repository after the project concludes. It is written for someone with no prior experience submitting research data. The process is designed to take no more than 30 minutes for a completed eight-week site.

Data submission is not optional. The project's ability to generate meaningful evidence depends on every completed site contributing its data to the pool. A site that runs the project but does not submit data has consumed the kit without contributing to it.

---

#### What You Are Submitting

At the end of the project you will submit three things:

**1. SWEMWBS data**
The anonymised scores from your baseline, mid-point, and post-project surveys. If you have been entering these into the Google Form after each survey round, this step is already done - confirm with the project coordinator that your submissions are showing correctly in the master Sheet.

If you collected paper forms but did not enter them during the project, enter them now using the Google Form described in Section 2.4 Document 4. Do this before anything else - it is the most time-sensitive data because paper forms can be lost or damaged.

**2. Site metadata**
Basic information about your site and how the project ran. This is submitted via a separate Google Form - the Site Completion Form - linked from the project WordPress site and from the QR code in Appendix L of the Facilitator Guide.

**3. Facilitator session notes**
Your completed session notes forms from each week. These are submitted as a single PDF scan or photograph set via the Site Completion Form.

You do not need to submit images - participants submit their own images directly during the project. The image submission Google Form feeds the same master Sheet as the SWEMWBS data.

---

#### The Site Completion Form

The Site Completion Form captures everything needed to contextualise your site's data in the pooled dataset. It is completed once, after Week 8, by the facilitator.

Fields:

*Site identification*
- Site code - assigned at registration, shown on your QR code cards
- Facilitator code
- Site name - the name of your town, suburb, or community. Does not need to be more specific than this
- Country and state/territory
- Location type - select from: metropolitan, regional, rural, remote
- Project start date and end date

*Cohort details*
- Youth cohort: number recruited, number who completed at least six of eight sessions, age range, primary recruitment pathway (e.g. drop-in centre, school, community notice)
- Older adult cohort: same fields
- Co-facilitator details for the youth cohort: role, organisation if any, Working With Children Check or equivalent held - yes/no

*Exhibition details*
- Venue type - select from: retail, library, community centre, health service, outdoor covered, other
- Venue name - optional, included in the public site registry only with venue's written permission
- Approximate weekly foot traffic - select from: under 50, 50-200, 200-500, over 500 per day. This is an estimate, not a measurement
- Week 8 closing event held - yes/no
- Approximate number of people attending the closing event

*Deviations from protocol*
- Were any of the eight weekly prompts modified? If yes, which weeks and what was the modified prompt
- Were any core design principles modified? If yes, which ones and why
- Any other significant deviations from the standard protocol

This field is important and should be completed honestly. Deviations are not failures - they are information. A site that modified Week 2's prompt for cultural reasons, or ran the project over ten weeks instead of eight due to a public holiday, contributes more useful information by documenting that deviation than by pretending it didn't happen.

*Open reflection*
- What worked well
- What you would do differently
- Any safety or safeguarding incidents - yes/no. If yes, a brief description. This field feeds a confidential review process, not the public dataset
- Would you run this project again - yes/no/maybe, with a free text field

---

#### What Happens to Your Data

**SWEMWBS data** is stored in the master Google Sheet, accessible only to the project coordinator. It is aggregated across sites before any analysis or publication. No individual site's data is reported in isolation without that site's facilitator's knowledge and agreement.

**Site metadata** is used in two ways. Non-identifying fields - location type, cohort sizes, exhibition details, deviation notes - are added to the public project dashboard on the WordPress site, which shows aggregate statistics across all completed sites. Identifying fields - site name, venue name - are included only with explicit permission.

**Session notes** are read by the project coordinator and used to inform kit improvements. They are not published or shared beyond the coordination team.

**Images** submitted by participants during the project are already in the system by the time the facilitator submits the Site Completion Form. They appear in the WordPress gallery tagged by site, week, prompt, and cohort. Participant captions appear alongside images. No participant names appear in the gallery.

---

#### Data Retention and Deletion

SWEMWBS data and site metadata are retained indefinitely for research purposes, in anonymised form. If a participant withdraws consent after the project concludes, their SWEMWBS data can be deleted from the master Sheet on request to the project coordinator - contact details in Appendix F of the Facilitator Guide.

Images can be removed from the WordPress gallery at any time on request from the participant or their facilitator. Removal from the gallery does not guarantee removal from any copies made by members of the public during the exhibition period - this limitation is disclosed in the consent form.

Paper consent forms and paper SWEMWBS forms should be retained by the facilitator for a minimum of two years after project completion, then destroyed securely. Digital copies should be stored in a password-protected location for the same period.

---

#### A Note on Research Publication

If you or a colleague wish to publish findings from your site's data in a peer-reviewed journal, you will need independent ethics approval for that publication. Running the project under this kit does not constitute ethics approval for research publication purposes.

The project coordinator maintains a register of sites interested in collaborative publication. If you are interested in contributing to a multi-site publication, note this in the open reflection field of the Site Completion Form. No commitment is implied by expressing interest.

The project's own publication pathway - a multi-site analysis of pooled SWEMWBS data and qualitative themes across the full dataset - will proceed when sufficient sites have completed the protocol to support meaningful analysis. Contributing sites will be acknowledged in any resulting publication. Named authorship for contributing facilitators will be considered on a case-by-case basis depending on the nature and extent of their contribution.

---

#### Submission Checklist

Before submitting, confirm:

- [ ] All SWEMWBS data entered into Google Form - baseline, mid-point, and post-project for all participants who completed each round
- [ ] Site Completion Form filled in completely, including deviation notes
- [ ] Session notes scanned or photographed and attached to Site Completion Form
- [ ] Week 8 closing event held, or documented reason why not
- [ ] Post-project SWEMWBS forms collected and entered
- [ ] Participant prints offered back to participants
- [ ] Paper consent forms and SWEMWBS forms stored securely
- [ ] Site registered as complete on the WordPress site project map

When all boxes are ticked, submit the Site Completion Form. You will receive an automated confirmation email. If you do not receive confirmation within 24 hours, contact the project coordinator.

---

#### Thank You

Running a *Through Two Sets of Eyes* project is a genuine contribution - to your community, and to the evidence base for intergenerational community arts as a health intervention. The data you submit will be combined with data from other sites around the world to build an argument that this kind of project is worth funding, worth replicating, and worth taking seriously.

The kit was designed to make this as easy as possible. If any part of the process was harder than it needed to be, say so in the open reflection field. The kit improves because facilitators tell us what doesn't work.
