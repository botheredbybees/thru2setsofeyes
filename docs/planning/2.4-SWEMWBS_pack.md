### 2.4 SWEMWBS Pack

---

#### Purpose

The SWEMWBS Pack gives facilitators everything they need to administer, score, and submit the Warwick-Edinburgh Mental Wellbeing Scale (Short Form) without any prior research or clinical experience. It does not modify the instrument - SWEMWBS is a validated psychometric tool and its questions, wording, and scoring must be used exactly as published.

---

#### What SWEMWBS Is

The Short Warwick-Edinburgh Mental Wellbeing Scale (SWEMWBS) is a seven-item self-report instrument measuring mental wellbeing in adults. It was developed by Stewart-Brown and Janmohamed (2008) and validated for use in community and population health settings. It is free to use for non-commercial purposes.

Each item describes a positive aspect of mental wellbeing and is rated on a five-point scale from "None of the time" to "All of the time" over the past two weeks. Raw scores are converted to metric scores using a published conversion table. The metric score range is 7 to 35, with higher scores indicating better wellbeing.

SWEMWBS is chosen for this project for four reasons:

- It is brief - seven items, typically completed in under three minutes
- It is validated for community settings and general adult populations, not just clinical groups
- It has published population norms allowing comparison across sites and against national data
- Validated translations exist in over 40 languages, supporting future multilingual versions of the kit

#### Pack Contents

**Document 1: Facilitator Administration Guide**

A plain-language guide covering:

- When to administer - baseline (Phase 0, before Week 1), mid-point (Week 4), and post-project (Week 8)
- How to distribute and collect paper forms - paper is the primary method for accessibility; the Google Form data entry option is for facilitator use only, not for participants
- How to explain the instrument to participants without influencing responses - a suggested script is provided
- What to do if a participant declines - note the refusal against the participant's anonymous code, do not pressure, proceed normally
- What to do if a participant needs help reading or understanding an item - read the item aloud without elaboration; do not explain or interpret individual items
- What to do if a participant becomes distressed during completion - stop, set the form aside, follow the distress response guidance in Facilitator Guide Section 5
- How to maintain anonymity - each participant is assigned a code at Phase 0 that they use on all three surveys, allowing pre-post matching without storing names against scores
- How to store completed paper forms securely between collection and data entry
- How to enter completed paper forms into the Google Form data entry interface on a phone or computer - one submission per participant per timepoint, done in bulk after each survey round

**Document 2: SWEMWBS Instrument - Participant Copy**

The seven SWEMWBS items formatted for self-completion, with the five-point response scale clearly displayed. Print-ready A4, one page.

Includes:
- The participant code field at the top with a brief explanation of why a code is used instead of a name
- The date field
- A brief introductory sentence in plain language
- The seven items exactly as published - wording must not be altered
- The response scale for each item
- A thank-you note at the bottom with a reminder that completion is voluntary

*Design note:* The form should be visually clean and unintimidating. Avoid anything that looks clinical.

---

**Document 3: Scoring Guide**

A plain-language explanation of how to score SWEMWBS, with the published raw-to-metric conversion table.

Covers:
- How to calculate the raw score - sum of the seven item responses
- How to convert raw score to metric score using the conversion table
- What the metric score means in plain terms
- How to handle missing items - one missing item can still be scored using published guidance; two or more missing items should be recorded as incomplete
- A worked example using fictional data

*Design note:* Facilitators do not need to understand the psychometric theory behind the conversion. The guide should be explicit that scores are for aggregate analysis only and are not feedback to give to individual participants.

---

**Document 4: Google Form Data Entry Guide**

A step-by-step guide for facilitators entering completed paper SWEMWBS forms into the project Google Form after each survey round.

Covers:
- How to access the form - via a QR code printed in this document, linking directly to the pre-configured form
- What each field means and where to find the information on the paper form
- How to submit one entry per participant - the form is designed for one participant per submission, not batch entry
- How to verify that submissions have been received - the Google Sheet confirmation view
- What to do if a submission was entered incorrectly - contact details for the project coordinator who can correct the master Sheet
- What to do if there is no internet connection - complete paper forms are the master record; enter data when connectivity is restored

The Google Form captures:
- Site code - pre-filled via the QR code link
- Facilitator code - entered once per session, not per participant
- Participant code
- Cohort - youth or older adult, select from list
- Timepoint - baseline, mid-point, or post-project, select from list
- Date of survey administration
- The seven raw item scores - entered as numbers 1-5
- A notes field for incomplete forms or unusual circumstances
- Form version number - displayed automatically, not entered by the facilitator

The metric score conversion is handled automatically in the linked Google Sheet - facilitators enter raw scores only.

**Document 5: Participant Code Generation Guide**

This guide explains how to create and remember a personal anonymous code that you will use on all three SWEMWBS surveys across the project. The code allows your responses to be matched across the three timepoints without your name being recorded anywhere.

**How to generate your code**

Your code is built from four pieces of personal information that only you know, combined in a fixed order:

1. The day of the month you were born - two digits. If you were born on the 4th, use 04.
2. The first two letters of your mother's first name - capitalised.
3. The last two letters of your own first name - capitalised.

**Example**

A participant born on the 14th of any month, whose mother's name is Margaret, and whose own name is Peter, would use:

`14MAER`

- 14 - day of birth
- MA - first two letters of Margaret
- ER - last two letters of Peter 


**The facilitator's script for Phase 0**

Read this aloud to participants at the orientation session:

> "I'm going to ask you to create a short personal code that only you will know. You'll use this code on three short surveys across the project - it means we can track how things change over time without your name being attached to anything. Here's how to make it:
>
> Take the day of the month you were born - so if you were born on the 5th, that's 05. Then take the first two letters of your mother's first name - capital letters. Then take the last two letters of your own first name - capital letters again. Put them together and that's your code.
>
> Take a moment now to work out your code and write it on this card. Keep the card somewhere safe - you'll need it again in Week 4 and Week 8."

Distribute the participant code cards (Appendix M - add this to the appendix list) at this point. The card has a brief reminder of the generation method and a space to write the code.

**What if a participant can't remember their code?**

If a participant arrives at Week 4 or Week 8 without their code card and cannot reconstruct it, use the following fallback method:

1. Ask the participant to reconstruct the code using the same rules
2. If they genuinely cannot, generate an alternative using: two-digit birth month + last two letters of their street name + first two letters of their first name
3. Note the fallback code against the original participant slot in your session notes, so the coordinator can match the records manually

Do not skip the SWEMWBS survey because a participant has lost their code - a matched record is better than a gap.

**Privacy note for facilitators**

You will see participants generate their codes at orientation. Do not write down or record the codes yourself - the whole point is that only the participant knows theirs. If a participant asks you to hold their code for them, explain that they should keep the card somewhere safe instead.

#### A Note on Sample Size

SWEMWBS data collected at a single site with six to sixteen participants is too small for inferential statistical analysis. The value of the data collection is cumulative - as sites are added, the pooled dataset grows toward a sample size that supports meaningful analysis. A suggested plain-language explanation for local funding bodies is provided in Facilitator Guide Section 7.
