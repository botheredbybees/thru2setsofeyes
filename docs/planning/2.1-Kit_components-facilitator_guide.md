# Through Two Sets of Eyes - Project Specification

## Part 2: Kit Components

### 2.1 Facilitator Guide

---

#### Purpose

The Facilitator Guide is the primary document a person uses to run a *Through Two Sets of Eyes* project. It assumes no arts background, no research experience, and no prior knowledge of Photovoice. It should be readable by a confident community volunteer who has never run a group arts project before.

The guide is not a script. It provides enough structure that a facilitator knows what to do in any situation, while leaving room for the project to go where the participants take it.

---

#### Format

- A single PDF, print-friendly, A4
- Sections clearly numbered so facilitators can find things quickly mid-session
- Plain language throughout - no academic terminology without explanation
- A quick-reference one-page summary at the front covering the eight-week arc, the core principles, and who to contact if something goes wrong
- Estimated reading time noted on the cover - the facilitator should know before they start that this is a two-hour read, not a ten-minute skim

---

#### Contents

**Section 1: What this project is and why it works**

A plain-language explanation of the project for a facilitator who knows nothing about it. Covers:

- What participants will do across eight weeks
- Why the two-cohort design matters - what it produces that a single-cohort project cannot
- Why the exhibition is unattributed until Week 8 - what that tension does for participants and for the community
- A brief, jargon-free explanation of Photovoice - where it comes from, what it's for, why it works for people who would never describe themselves as artists
- A brief, honest statement of what the evidence says and what it doesn't say yet - including the fact that this specific design is new and the project is designed to generate evidence over time

**Section 2: Before you start - Phase 0**

Everything that needs to be in place before Week 1. Written as a checklist with explanatory notes for each item:

- Finding and confirming your exhibition venue - what to look for, what questions to ask, what a contingency venue is and why you need one
- Recruiting participants - suggested approaches for each cohort, minimum and ideal group sizes, what to do if recruitment is slow
- The loan device - why one is needed, what to source, how to set it up
- Distributing baseline SWEMWBS surveys - when, how, and what to do if someone doesn't complete one
- The orientation session - what to cover, how long it should take, what materials to bring
- Consent - who needs to sign what, when, and what happens if a participant under 18 cannot get guardian consent before Week 1
- Confirming your co-facilitator for the younger cohort - why this is not optional, what their role is, what the minimum requirement is for their involvement
- The Phase 0 completion checklist - the project does not start until every item is ticked

**Section 3: Running the sessions - Weeks 1 to 8**

A week-by-week guide. Each week follows the same structure:

- *What this week is for* - one paragraph on the purpose of this week in the arc of the project
- *What to prepare before the session* - materials, printed prompts, any specific setup
- *Session outline* - a timed 90-minute structure the facilitator can follow or adapt
- *What to watch for* - specific things that commonly happen at this stage, and how to respond
- *After the session* - image selection, printing, installation, and notes to complete

The session outline for a typical week (Weeks 3-7) looks like this:

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0-10 min | Welcome and settling - same opening ritual every week |
| 10-40 min | Image sharing - each participant shows one to three images without explanation; group looks in silence before anyone speaks |
| 40-65 min | Discussion - participant reflects on what they chose and why; others respond; facilitator holds the space |
| 65-80 min | Editorial selection - group selects one image per participant for that week's exhibition |
| 80-90 min | Next week's prompt - presented as a suggestion, participants invited to revise or replace it |

Weeks 1, 2, 4, and 8 have modified structures noted separately.

**Section 4: The exhibition**

How to set up and maintain the exhibition across eight weeks. Covers:

- What makes a good exhibition venue - foot traffic, visibility, accessibility, weather protection for prints
- Print specifications - recommended size, paper, and print source options including home printing
- Installation - how to pair and label images, what the pairing logic is, how to handle the accumulation across weeks
- The QR code - how to generate one linking to the project gallery, how to display it
- What to do if the venue becomes unavailable mid-project

These subjects are covered in more detail in the [Exhibition Guide](2.5-Exhibition_guide.md) document

**Section 5: Looking after participants**

The most important section for a facilitator with no prior community arts experience. Covers:

- The difference between emotion that needs to be witnessed and emotion that needs care staff support - and how to tell which is which. The guide should be frank that most distress in a well-run session is the first kind - a participant crying while describing a photograph is usually not a crisis. The facilitator's job is to hold the space, not to intervene or fix. But the guide must also be clear about what the second kind looks like: escalating distress that doesn't settle, disclosure of ongoing harm, a participant whose affect is significantly disconnected from the situation.

- Disclosure protocol - covering three distinct situations the facilitator may encounter:

  *Verbal in-session disclosure:* A participant tells the facilitator, during or after a session, something concerning - domestic violence, self-harm, abuse, a safeguarding concern involving someone else. The guide should give four clear instructions: (1) listen and acknowledge without dismissing; (2) do not promise confidentiality you cannot keep - if there is a mandatory reporting obligation, the facilitator will need to act on it, and saying "this is just between us" before knowing that is a breach of trust the facilitator cannot repair; (3) do not probe for more detail than the participant volunteers - the facilitator is not an investigator; (4) tell the participant what you plan to do before doing it, where possible - "I need to speak to [local safeguarding contact] about this - I want you to know that before I do." Then do it. Document privately - not in the online session notes form, not in any shared document.

  *Image-based disclosure:* A participant submits or presents an image that shows evidence of harm - self-harm indicators, injuries, a situation that raises immediate concern about their safety or someone else's. Two scenarios: the facilitator sees it during the group session (the participant shares and discusses it), or the coordinator sees it in the moderation queue. For the facilitator during a session: do not exhibit the image; respond to the participant as a person first, not to the image as a safeguarding problem. For the coordinator in moderation: follow the HANDOVER.md protocol - contact the facilitator directly, do not exhibit, document privately.

  *Behavioural signals:* A participant stops attending without explanation, or attends but shows a significant change in presentation or affect. This is the hardest category because there is no disclosure to respond to - only a signal. The guide should instruct the facilitator to reach out warmly after one missed session, not two - a gentle check-in from someone with an existing relationship, not a welfare call from a stranger. For the youth cohort, the co-facilitator with the existing relationship should make this contact. The guide should be clear that the facilitator is not a crisis service and should not position themselves as one - the goal is to make sure the participant knows they are welcome back, not to investigate what is happening at home.

- Mandatory reporting obligations - a plain statement that this guide cannot cover every jurisdiction, and that the facilitator must know their local obligations before Week 1. Appendix F asks each facilitator to fill in their local emergency, safeguarding, and mental health support contacts before the project starts. This is not optional. A facilitator who encounters a disclosure and has to search for the right number in the moment has already failed the participant.
- The younger cohort - why a co-facilitator with an existing relationship with participants is non-negotiable, and what the project lead's boundaries are around solo contact
- Withdrawal - a participant's right to withdraw any image at any time, and how to handle a mid-project withdrawal from the exhibition
- Looking after yourself as a facilitator - brief and practical, not performative

**Section 6: Data collection**

A plain-language guide to the three data streams, written for someone who has never collected research data:

- SWEMWBS - what it is, why it's used, how to administer it at baseline, Week 4, and Week 8, what to do if a participant declines, how to score it, how to enter and submit the data
- Facilitator session notes - what to record, when, and why; the one-page template is in Appendix B
- Reflection cards - when to distribute, how to collect, what to do with them

A clear statement that participation in data collection is voluntary for participants, and that a participant who declines to complete SWEMWBS can still participate fully in the project.

**Section 7: After the project**

- Submitting data to the central portal - step by step
- Uploading images to the project gallery
- The summary report - a one-page optional template for sites that want to report findings to a local council, funding body, or community organisation
- What happens to the exhibition after Week 8
- How to register a completed site on the project map

**Section 8: If something goes wrong**

A short, honest section covering the most likely practical failures and their solutions:

- Participant dropout below minimum group size
- Exhibition venue unavailable
- Co-facilitator unavailable
- A participant makes a serious disclosure
- A photograph raises a child protection concern
- A conflict arises between participants
- The Week 8 reveal surfaces unexpected emotion or tension between cohorts
- The project lead becomes unable to continue mid-project

Each scenario gets a brief description of the recommended response and, where relevant, a pointer to external resources.

The "A participant makes a serious disclosure" scenario should cross-reference Section 5's disclosure protocol and add the following: a disclosure does not end the participant's place in the group unless the participant wants it to. The facilitator should not withdraw warmth, change how they treat the participant in sessions, or make the participant feel that disclosing was a mistake. The most common error a well-intentioned facilitator makes after a disclosure is over-attending to the participant — checking in too frequently, treating them differently from the others, making the disclosure the defining feature of the relationship. The guide should name this explicitly and advise against it.

The "A photograph raises a child protection concern" scenario should distinguish between the two discovery pathways: the facilitator seeing the image in-session versus the coordinator seeing it in the moderation queue. In-session: the facilitator does not exhibit the image but also does not call out the participant in front of the group. After the session, they make contact privately, follow the mandatory reporting protocol for their jurisdiction, and notify the coordinator. The coordinator does not need to be involved in the mandatory report itself — that is the facilitator's obligation, not the coordinator's. The coordinator's role is image moderation: do not exhibit, document the action in `moderation_notes`, and support the facilitator if they raise questions.

The Week 8 reveal scenario should note that the project spec describes this event as requiring no structured facilitation, and that is usually true — but that the facilitator should be present and available, not absent. Specific situations to prepare for: one cohort whose work is visibly more developed or numerous than the other's; a participant who is distressed to discover who made a particular image; a participant whose response to seeing the other cohort is hostile or dismissive. The recommended response in all cases is to hold the space without directing it — the facilitator's job is to ensure everyone can leave with dignity, not to engineer a warm resolution. A brief "grounding close" — a few minutes where each participant says one thing they are taking away — is the lightest possible facilitation intervention and can absorb most awkward endings.

The section closes with a reminder that the GitHub Issues page is the right place to raise situations not covered here.

#### Design Notes

The guide should be written in second person throughout - "you" not "the facilitator." It should feel like a trusted colleague explaining something, not a policy document.

Every instruction that could go wrong in an unfamiliar way should include a "what if" note. A first-time facilitator's biggest fear is not knowing what to do when something unexpected happens - the guide should anticipate that anxiety and address it directly.

The guide should be honest about uncertainty. If the evidence for a design decision is strong, say so briefly. If it's a judgement call, say that too. Facilitators who understand why the project is designed the way it is will make better decisions when the guide doesn't cover their specific situation.
